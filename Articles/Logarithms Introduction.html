Logarithms: Introduction

Introduction

Logarithms (commonly referred to as log) are mathematical equations that are generally known as the opposite of exponentiation. A logarithmic equation always has the same fundamentals…

log10(x) = 2

Logarithms sometimes have variables with potential solutions. Take a look at this example. We can convert this logarithm into an equation…

log10(x) = 2

Move the ten on the left over to the other side...

x = 102

Now the small number on the right of the log is the base...

x = 10

Now the two that just got erased is the exponent...

x = 102

Now we could just replace the 10 to the power of 2 with 100...

x = 100

And the final answer of this logarithmic equation is 100. Typically the base of the log equation is 10…

log(100) = 2

If there isn’t a base in the log equation it is most likely 10.

log10(100) = 2

100 = 102

100 = 100

This isn’t a false logarithm which means that the two numbers are equal.

Sometimes when calculating log equations, they sometimes don’t have solutions…

log3(28) = 4

28 = 34

28 ≠ 81

Since these two numbers are not equal, this log equation doesn’t have a solution.

Log equations also sometimes have variables like x…

log5(5x) = 4

Since this log equation has a variable in it, there is a solution because x could be any value...

5x = 54

5x = 625

Divide both sides by 5

x = 125

Alternatively divide at the start.

x = 53

x = 125
